### §1. Purpose and Scope

This document defines the social contract, contribution guidelines,
ethical boundaries, communication standards, and licensing model
for the antiphoria slop-research-factory as an open-source project.

Unlike the preceding documents (D-1 through D-6), which specify
technical architecture, D-7 specifies **human behavior**. It
governs how people talk about the project, what claims they may
and may not make, how contributions are evaluated, and what the
project exists to accomplish in the world.

**What this document decides:**
- The project's mission statement, including the explicit "slop"
  designation and its social function.
- The Code of Conduct for contributors and users.
- Communication standards: what language is permitted and
  prohibited in official project communications.
- Contribution acceptance criteria and review process.
- The licensing model and intellectual property framework.
- The relationship between this project and the academic
  publishing ecosystem.
- The project's position on the Autonomous Mathematics Research
  Levels taxonomy (Feng et al., 2026b, §6.1).
- The governance structure for decision-making.

**What this document does NOT decide:**
- Technical architecture (D-0 through D-5).
- Prompt text (D-3).
- HAI Card content (D-6, which defines the template; D-7 defines
  the social norms around its use).

**Key upstream constraint from D-1 §10:**
> "The charter must prohibit contributors from marketing the
> provenance chain as a guarantee of scientific correctness.
> The project's public communications must consistently use the
> phrase 'process provenance' and never 'verified research.'"

### §2. Mission Statement

#### 2.1 What the Project Is

The antiphoria slop-research-factory is an open-source tool that
automates the production of academic-style research documents
using a cryptographically auditable pipeline of large language
model inference calls.

It exists to accomplish three things:

**1. Demonstrate that process provenance is technically feasible
for AI-generated research.**

No tool currently available to independent researchers provides
a cryptographic proof of how an AI-generated document was
produced. The factory is the first open-source implementation
of a full hash-chain provenance system integrated into a
Generator-Verifier-Reviser research loop. By building it, we
prove that provenance is not a theoretical ideal but an
engineering problem with a concrete solution.

**2. Force an honest conversation about AI-generated research.**

The academic community is experiencing an epistemological
crisis. AI-generated text is entering the scientific literature
at accelerating rates — estimated at 13.5% of abstracts in
2024 (research-factory-deep-research.txt, §1), with
hallucinated citations surging ninefold to 2.6% in 2025. The
response from publishers has been defensive: blanket bans,
disclosure checklists, and prohibition of AI authorship. The
response from AI companies has been promotional: showcasing
success stories while obscuring failure rates.

Neither response is adequate. The factory exists to occupy the
honest middle ground: **here is what an AI research pipeline
actually produces, with every step sealed and auditable, so
that the community can evaluate the output on its merits —
or lack thereof.**

**3. Build the infrastructure that future, better models will need.**

The factory's architecture — the cyclic graph, the seal
engine, the verification taxonomy, the HAI Card — is
model-agnostic. Today's models produce slop. Tomorrow's models
may produce real research. When they do, the provenance
infrastructure must already exist. We are building the
plumbing before the water is clean.

#### 2.2 What the Project Is Not

The project is **not**:

- A claim that AI can do research.
- A replacement for human scientists.
- A shortcut for academic publication.
- A product for generating papers to submit to journals
  without human domain-expert review.
- A competitor to Google DeepMind's Aletheia or any
  proprietary research system.

The project is an **instrument of radical transparency** in
a domain drowning in opacity.

#### 2.3 The "Slop" Designation

The word "slop" in the project name is **deliberate,
load-bearing, and non-negotiable**.

**Etymology:** In internet culture, "slop" refers to
AI-generated content that mimics the surface structure of
authentic human production while lacking the underlying
substance. A slop article reads like journalism but contains
no reporting. A slop paper reads like research but contains
no discovery.

**Why we use it:**

1. **Honesty.** Google DeepMind's Aletheia — the most
   advanced autonomous mathematics research system ever
   built — achieved a meaningful-correctness rate of 6.5%
   on Erdős problems (Feng et al., 2026b, Table 6). Its
   outputs required evaluation by panels of named
   mathematicians at top universities. Our system uses
   weaker models, has no access to proprietary inference
   infrastructure, and replaces human expert verification
   with an LLM Verifier whose confidence ceiling is 0.6 on
   logical tasks (D-4 §3.2). The honest assessment of our
   output quality is: slop.

2. **Inoculation.** By naming the output "slop" ourselves,
   we prevent downstream consumers from over-interpreting it.
   A document produced by the "slop-research-factory" cannot
   easily be cited as "AI-verified research" without the
   citer actively overriding the explicit warning in the name.
   The name is a social-engineering defense against the
   epistemic free-rider attack described in D-1 §3
   (Adversary 5).

3. **Aspiration.** The "slop" label is not permanent. It is a
   version tag on the state of the art. When the verification
   taxonomy (D-4) evolves to include formal proof checking,
   when provider-side response signing becomes available,
   when the confidence ceilings rise above 0.8 on logical
   tasks — then the community will have earned the right to
   drop the word "slop." Until then, it stays.

**The "slop" designation may be removed only by a
supermajority (75%) vote of the project's Technical Steering
Committee (§11), based on empirical evidence that the system's
output has crossed a defined quality threshold (§2.4).**

#### 2.4 Conditions for Removing the "Slop" Designation

The following conditions must ALL be met before the project
may be renamed to "research-factory" (without the "slop"
prefix):

| # | Condition | Measurement |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | At least one factory output has been accepted for publication in a peer-reviewed venue (not JAIGP) after independent human expert review. | Documented acceptance letter or DOI. |
| 2 | The Verification Taxonomy (D-4) includes at least one T1 (deterministic) task for logical/mathematical verification. | D-4 version history shows a T1 task in the LM-* category. |
| 3 | The average `verdict_confidence` on a standardized evaluation suite exceeds 0.8 at default settings. | Benchmark results published in the project repository. |
| 4 | Provider-side response signing (D-1 §7) is integrated for at least one LLM provider. | D-5 version history shows provider signature verification. |
| 5 | The project has operated for at least 12 months under the "slop" designation without any documented case of a downstream consumer successfully misrepresenting factory output as "verified research." | Incident log in the project repository. |

These conditions are deliberately high bars. The "slop"
designation is a feature, not a bug.

### §3. The Social Contract

#### 3.1 Between the Project and Its Users

The project makes the following promises to its users:

| Promise | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| The output is honest about what it is. | The "slop" name, the disclaimer (D-6 §4.1), the HAI Card (D-6), and the security guarantee (D-1 §9) are all mandatory, non-removable output artifacts. |
| The process is fully auditable. | The provenance chain (D-5) seals every step. The verification report (D-0 §9.4) visualizes it. |
| The project never overclaims. | This charter (§5) defines prohibited language. Violations by maintainers result in removal. |
| The project is free to use, modify, and redistribute. | The license (§12) guarantees this. |

The project makes the following demands of its users:

| Demand | Justification |
|---|---|
| Do not remove the disclaimer from output artifacts. | The disclaimer is part of the social contract. Removing it and publishing the output as unqualified research violates the license (§12.2). |
| Do not claim factory output is "verified" or "certified." | The provenance chain verifies process, not truth (D-1 §9). Misrepresenting this harms the entire community. |
| Do submit factory output to human domain experts before citing it in any context where scientific accuracy matters. | The factory is a drafting tool, not a validation tool. |
| Do publish the full workspace (including provenance chain) alongside any public use of factory output. | Selective disclosure defeats the purpose of provenance (D-1 §6, Non-Defense 3). |

#### 3.2 Between the Project and the Academic Community

The project acknowledges:

1. **AI cannot currently do research.** The factory produces
   structured text that resembles research. Resemblance is
   not equivalence. The gap between Aletheia's 6.5%
   meaningful-correctness rate on Erdős problems and the
   standards of human mathematical research is vast.

2. **The factory does not replace peer review.** The Verifier
   node is a pre-screening filter with known, documented
   blind spots (D-4 §12). It is not a peer reviewer. It is
   not a substitute for a peer reviewer. It does not claim
   to be a peer reviewer.

3. **Human accountability is non-negotiable.** Following the
   universal publisher policy (research-factory-deep-research.txt,
   §"The Absolute Ban on Non-Human Authorship"): the factory
   is never listed as an author. The human operator bears
   total accountability for any use of factory output.

4. **The provenance chain is a tool for transparency, not
   a credential.** The chain proves process. It does not
   prove truth, novelty, or significance. Any representation
   to the contrary — by any party, including project
   maintainers — is a violation of this charter.

The project requests:

1. That the academic community engage with the question of
   AI-generated research provenance rather than ignoring it.
   The factory exists to make the conversation concrete.

2. That publishers consider defining provenance standards
   for AI-assisted submissions, rather than relying solely
   on honor-system disclosure checklists.

3. That the mathematical community continue developing the
   Autonomous Mathematics Research Levels taxonomy
   (Feng et al., 2026b, §6.1) as a shared vocabulary for
   describing AI contributions.

#### 3.3 Between the Project and the Media

The project has observed the pattern described in Feng et al.
(2026b, §6):

> "This evaluation gap has enabled a surge in hyperbolic or
> misleading messaging regarding AI-generated mathematical
> content."

And the specific case documented in the deep research report
(research-factory-deep-research.txt, §"Sakana AI"):

> "Widespread media reports erroneously claimed that an
> artificial intelligence had independently authored a paper
> published in the prestigious journal [Nature]."

To prevent similar misrepresentation of this project:

1. **All project press releases, blog posts, and social media
   communications must be reviewed by at least two members
   of the Technical Steering Committee before publication.**

2. **The word "slop" must appear in the first paragraph of
   any official project communication.**

3. **No official communication may use the phrases "AI
   scientist," "AI researcher," "autonomous discovery," or
   "AI-generated research" without immediately qualifying
   them as aspirational descriptions of a future state, not
   a current capability.**

4. **Journalists are directed to cite the security guarantee
   (D-1 §9) verbatim when describing the provenance system.**

### §4. Positioning Within the Autonomous Mathematics
Research Levels Taxonomy

Feng et al. (2026b, §6.1) propose a two-axis taxonomy for
AI-assisted mathematics. We adopt this taxonomy as the
project's canonical framework for describing its output.

#### 4.1 Autonomy Axis

All factory output is **Level A (Essentially Autonomous)**
by definition: the human provides only the research brief
(D-2 §5), and everything else is generated by the pipeline.

However, we note a crucial distinction between our Level A
and Aletheia's Level A:

| Attribute | Aletheia Level A | Factory Level A |
|---|---|---|
| Model | Gemini 3 Deep Think (frontier) | DeepSeek-R1 (open-weights) |
| Verifier | Same frontier model | Gemini Flash (weaker, different model) |
| Tool access | Google Search, web browsing (trained for tool use) | Tavily, Crossref, Semantic Scholar (API-only) |
| Human evaluation | Panels of named mathematicians at top universities | None (unless human rescue triggered) |
| Self-filtering | Returns "No output" when uncertain | Returns "NO_OUTPUT" when uncertain (same design principle) |
| Institutional credibility | Google DeepMind | None |

The factory's Level A output is **categorically weaker** than
Aletheia's Level A output due to weaker models, weaker
verification, and no human expert evaluation. The "slop"
designation reflects this difference.

#### 4.2 Significance Axis

Per D-6 §8: the factory does NOT self-assign a significance
level. The HAI Card always reports:
Significance: [TO BE ASSESSED BY HUMAN DOMAIN EXPERT]
This is non-negotiable. Per Feng et al. (2026b, §6.1.2):

> "The Level of Mathematical Significance can only be
> evaluated by mathematicians."

The factory has no mechanism to assess whether its output is
Level 0 (trivial) or Level 1 (minor novelty). We **expect**
Level 0 at the current state of the art. If a human domain
expert later assesses a factory output as Level 1 or above,
that assessment is theirs, not the factory's.

#### 4.3 The Path from Slop to Science

The Autonomous Mathematics Research Levels taxonomy implies a
progression. The factory is designed to make that progression
legible:
TODAY (v0.x):
Autonomy: A
Significance: 0 (expected)
Verification: T3 ceiling (LLM-only, 0.6 max)
Name: slop-research-factory
NEAR FUTURE (v1.x):
Autonomy: A
Significance: 0–1 (possible)
Verification: T2 ceiling (tool-assisted, 0.8 max)
Name: slop-research-factory (still)
New capabilities: formal constraint checking,
multi-source citation verification, provider signing
FAR FUTURE (v2.x):
Autonomy: A
Significance: 1–2 (aspirational)
Verification: T1 available (deterministic, 1.0)
Name: research-factory (slop removed per §2.4)
New capabilities: Lean 4 integration, sandboxed code
execution, full-text citation verification
This progression is not a product roadmap. It is a
hypothesis about what would need to be true for the output
to stop being slop. The conditions in §2.4 operationalize
this hypothesis.

### §5. Communication Standards

#### 5.1 Mandatory Language

The following phrases MUST appear in all official project
communications (README, website, conference talks, blog
posts, social media):

| Context | Required Phrase |
|---|---|
| First mention of the project | "The antiphoria slop-research-factory" (full name, including "slop") |
| Describing what the provenance chain proves | "process provenance" |
| Describing the output quality | "highly polished slop" or "structured text that resembles research" |
| Describing the Verifier | "an automated pre-screening filter" |
| Describing the HAI Card | "a disclosure template, not a quality certification" |
| Describing the project's purpose | "radical transparency in AI-generated research" |

#### 5.2 Prohibited Language

The following phrases MUST NOT appear in any official project
communication, including commit messages, issue titles, PR
descriptions, documentation, talks, and social media:

| Prohibited Phrase | Why | Acceptable Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "verified research" | Implies the provenance chain verifies scientific truth. It does not. (D-1 §9) | "auditable output" or "provenance-sealed output" |
| "certified" or "certification" | Implies a credential. The chain is evidence, not a credential. | "sealed" or "chained" |
| "AI scientist" | Implies the system possesses scientific judgment. It does not. | "research drafting pipeline" or "generation pipeline" |
| "autonomous discovery" | Implies the system can discover things. Current evidence says otherwise. | "autonomous generation" |
| "trustworthy" (applied to output) | Implies the output can be trusted. It cannot, without human review. | "auditable" or "transparent" |
| "publication-ready" | Implies the output meets publication standards. It does not. | "structured draft" or "candidate draft" |
| "peer-reviewed" (applied to the Verifier) | The Verifier is not a peer reviewer. | "pre-screened" or "automatically verified (with known limitations)" |
| "breakthrough" or "novel result" | Implies the system has achieved a scientific milestone. Only human domain experts can make this determination. | "output" or "draft" |
| "proves" (applied to scientific claims) | The chain proves process. The system proves nothing scientifically. | "generates" or "proposes" |

#### 5.3 Enforcement

Violations of §5.1 or §5.2 are handled as follows:

- **In code/documentation:** Any contributor may flag a
  violation in a PR review. The violating text must be
  corrected before merge.
- **In official communications:** Any TSC member may request
  a correction. The correction must be issued within 48
  hours. Repeated violations by a TSC member result in a
  governance review (§11.4).
- **By third parties (media, users):** The project cannot
  control third-party language, but it MUST issue a public
  correction when aware of a significant misrepresentation.
  The correction is posted to the project's official
  channels and linked from the README.

### §6. Code of Conduct

#### 6.1 Scope

This Code of Conduct applies to all interactions in project
spaces: the code repository, issue tracker, discussion
forums, chat channels, mailing lists, conferences, and any
other venue where a person represents the project.

#### 6.2 Principles

1. **Intellectual honesty above all.** The project exists
   because the AI-research ecosystem lacks honesty. We do
   not tolerate dishonesty within the project itself. If
   you discover a flaw in the architecture, a failure mode
   in the Verifier, or a gap in the threat model — report
   it. Do not conceal it.

2. **Respect for the scientific community.** Many scientists
   are alarmed by AI-generated research. Their alarm is
   justified. We engage with their concerns seriously and
   do not dismiss them as Luddism.

3. **Respect for contributors.** We welcome contributions
   from researchers, engineers, students, and anyone who
   shares the project's commitment to transparency. We do
   not gatekeep based on institutional affiliation, formal
   credentials, or prior publication record.

4. **No hype.** The project's value proposition is honesty,
   not capability. We do not exaggerate what the system can
   do. We do not chase attention by overclaiming. If a
   contributor's PR description contains prohibited language
   (§5.2), the reviewer should correct it collegially, not
   punitively.

5. **No weaponization.** The factory must not be used to
   generate content intended to deceive: fake papers
   submitted as genuine human work, fabricated evidence,
   or spam targeting academic venues. See §8 (Ethical
   Boundaries) for specifics.

#### 6.3 Violations

Violations of the Code of Conduct are reported to the
project maintainers via a dedicated email address. Reports
are handled confidentially. The response follows a graduated
process:

1. **First violation (minor):** Private conversation with the
   contributor. Correction of the offending content.
2. **Second violation or first serious violation:** Public
   warning in the relevant project space.
3. **Third violation or first severe violation:** Temporary
   ban from all project spaces (duration: 30–90 days).
4. **Continued or egregious violations:** Permanent ban.

"Severe violations" include: submitting factory output to an
academic venue without disclosure, removing the disclaimer
from published output, or deliberately misrepresenting the
provenance chain as a scientific validation.

### §7. Contribution Guidelines

#### 7.1 What We Welcome

| Contribution Type | Examples | Review Process |
|---|---|---|
| **Bug fixes** | Crash in seal wrapper, incorrect hash computation, Pydantic validation error | Standard PR review by 1 maintainer |
| **Verification improvements** | New T1 or T2 tasks for D-4, improved citation checking, new tool integrations | PR review by 2 maintainers + D-4 version update |
| **Model support** | New model integrations via LiteLLM, local model support via Ollama | PR review by 1 maintainer |
| **Documentation** | Tutorials, deployment guides, translated documentation | PR review by 1 maintainer |
| **Prompt improvements** | Modifications to D-3 prompts with empirical evidence of improvement | PR review by 2 maintainers + D-3 CHANGELOG entry |
| **Provenance enhancements** | RFC 3161 timestamping, run registry, provider-signing integration | PR review by 2 maintainers + D-1/D-5 version update |
| **Evaluation infrastructure** | Benchmark suites, calibration datasets, automated regression tests | PR review by 2 maintainers |
| **Research using the factory** | Papers, blog posts, or reports that use the factory and publish the full workspace | No PR required; linked from the project's "Gallery" page |

#### 7.2 What We Do Not Welcome

| Contribution Type | Why | Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Removing or weakening the disclaimer | Violates the social contract (§3.1) | Propose improvements to the disclaimer text instead |
| Removing or bypassing provenance sealing | Violates the Glass Box invariant (D-0 §3) | Propose performance improvements to the seal engine instead |
| Making the "slop" designation configurable | The name is a social-engineering defense, not a cosmetic choice (§2.3) | Contribute to the conditions for removal (§2.4) instead |
| Adding autonomous submission to journals | The factory must never submit papers without human intervention | Build tools that help humans submit, not tools that replace them |
| "AI scientist" branding | Prohibited language (§5.2) | Use the approved terminology |
| Closed-source model integrations that prevent workspace auditing | Contradicts radical transparency | Ensure all model responses are captured and sealed, regardless of the model's license |

#### 7.3 Contribution Quality Standards

All code contributions must:

1. **Pass type checking** (`mypy --strict` or equivalent).
2. **Include tests** for new functionality.
3. **Not introduce new dependencies** without TSC approval.
4. **Document any deviation** from the architecture specified
   in D-0 through D-6, with justification.
5. **Update the relevant D-document** if the contribution
   changes the system's behavior in a way that contradicts
   the current specification.

All prompt contributions (D-3) must:

1. **Include empirical evidence** that the change improves
   output quality on at least one test from D-3 §8.1.
2. **Not introduce prohibited language** (§5.2) into the
   prompts themselves.
3. **Record the change** in `prompts/CHANGELOG.md` with
   date, motivation, and test results.

#### 7.4 Attribution

Contributors are credited in the project's `CONTRIBUTORS.md`
file. Contributions are attributed to individuals, not to
institutions or AI systems. An AI system cannot be a
contributor to this project. If a contributor used an AI
coding assistant to write their contribution, they should
note this in the PR description for transparency, but the
human contributor retains full attribution and accountability.

### §8. Ethical Boundaries

#### 8.1 Permitted Uses

The factory may be used for:

1. **Exploratory drafting.** Generating structured drafts on
   research topics as a starting point for human researchers.
2. **Provenance research.** Studying the properties of
   cryptographic provenance chains for AI-generated content.
3. **Verification research.** Studying the capabilities and
   limitations of LLM-based verification.
4. **Education.** Teaching students about AI-generated
   content, provenance, and the epistemology of automated
   science.
5. **Artistic exploration.** The factory's output is, by the
   project's own admission, an aesthetic object — text that
   resembles research without being research. Exploring
   this resemblance as a cultural phenomenon is a legitimate
   use.
6. **Submission to JAIGP or equivalent AI-native venues.**
   Provided the full workspace is published alongside the
   submission and the HAI Card is included.
7. **Submission to traditional venues as AI-assisted work.**
   Provided a human domain expert has reviewed and taken
   accountability for the scientific claims, the full
   workspace is available, and the venue's AI disclosure
   requirements are met.

#### 8.2 Prohibited Uses

The factory MUST NOT be used for:

1. **Fraudulent submission.** Submitting factory output to an
   academic venue as human-authored work, without disclosure
   of AI involvement.
2. **Credential fabrication.** Using factory output to
   fabricate academic credentials, inflate publication
   counts, or game citation metrics.
3. **Disinformation.** Generating plausible-looking "research"
   to support false claims in public discourse (e.g.,
   fabricated medical studies, fake climate science).
4. **Spam.** Bulk-generating papers for submission to
   predatory journals or conference proceedings.
5. **Peer review manipulation.** Using factory output to
   generate or influence peer reviews.
6. **Provenance laundering.** Using the provenance chain to
   lend false credibility to human-authored content by
   running it through the factory and claiming it was
   "AI-generated with full provenance" (D-1 §3, Adversary 2).

#### 8.3 The Responsibility Boundary

The project provides a tool. The project does not control
how the tool is used. The following principle governs the
project's responsibility:

> **The project is responsible for the tool's honesty
> (§3.1). The user is responsible for the tool's use
> (§3.2).**

The project guarantees that the factory, when run with
default settings on unmodified code, produces output that
is honestly labeled, fully auditable, and accompanied by
appropriate disclaimers. The project does NOT guarantee that
users will run unmodified code, publish full workspaces, or
include disclaimers.

This is the same trust model as any other open-source tool.
A cryptographic library does not guarantee that its users
will use it ethically. A word processor does not guarantee
that its users will not fabricate data. The factory's
contribution is to make the honest path easier than the
dishonest path — but it cannot force honesty.

### §9. Relationship to Aletheia and Proprietary Systems

#### 9.1 Intellectual Debt

This project owes a substantial intellectual debt to Google
DeepMind's Aletheia papers:

- **The Generator-Verifier-Reviser loop** is directly
  modeled on Aletheia's subagent topology (Feng et al.,
  2026b, Figure 1).
- **The Verification-and-Extraction prompt** (D-3 §4) is
  adapted from Aletheia's published prompt (Feng et al.,
  2026a, Appendix A).
- **The HAI Card** (D-6) is modeled on the HAI Card concept
  introduced in Feng et al. (2026b, §6.2).
- **The self-filtering mechanism** (`NO_OUTPUT`) is directly
  inspired by Aletheia's practice of returning "No solution
  found" (Feng et al., 2026a, §3.1).
- **The Autonomous Mathematics Research Levels** taxonomy
  (§4) is adopted from Feng et al. (2026b, §6.1).

This debt is acknowledged transparently. The Aletheia papers
are cited in every relevant D-document and in the project
README. The project does not claim to be Aletheia or to
achieve Aletheia-level performance.

#### 9.2 What We Learned from Aletheia

| Aletheia Finding | Our Architectural Response |
|---|---|
| "Decoupling a reasoning model's final output from its intermediate thinking tokens... enables the model to recognize flaws it initially overlooked" (2026b, §2.2) | The Reviser receives only the Verifier's structured critique, never its own think trace or the Verifier's think trace (D-3 §5.3). |
| "Aletheia often admits failure to solve a problem, a key feature" (2026b, §2.2) | `NO_OUTPUT` is a first-class terminal state with its own sealed provenance chain (D-2 §3.3, D-3 §7). |
| "Simply granting internet access is insufficient" (2026b, §2.3) | The Verifier uses structured tool APIs (Crossref, Semantic Scholar) rather than unstructured web search for citation checking (D-4 §3.1). |
| "Out of 200 solution candidates... only 13 (6.5%) were meaningfully correct" (2026b, Table 6) | We do not expect meaningful correctness from our output. The "slop" designation acknowledges this. |
| "The model exhibits a tendency to misinterpret the question in a way that is easiest to answer" (2026b, §5.3) | The scope compliance checks (D-4 §3.3, SC-1 and SC-2) attempt to catch this, but at T3 confidence ceiling. |
| "Citation errors shifted to subtler hallucinations, where the reference exists but its results are not quoted properly" (2026b, §2.3, Figure 4) | The CV-5 (claim-citation relevance) task uses abstract retrieval to detect this failure mode, though at T2 confidence ceiling (D-4 §3.1). |
| "Even with its verifier mechanism, Aletheia is still more prone to errors than human experts" (2026b, §5.3) | The entire D-4 document is an extended acknowledgment of this fact, with explicit confidence ceilings and blind spot documentation. |

#### 9.3 What We Cannot Learn from Aletheia

| Aletheia Advantage | Why We Cannot Replicate It |
|---|---|
| Frontier models (Gemini 3 Deep Think) | Proprietary, not available via API with equivalent capabilities. We use DeepSeek-R1 and Gemini Flash. |
| Panels of named mathematicians for evaluation | We have no institutional affiliation and no budget for expert evaluation panels. The Verifier LLM is our only evaluator. |
| Institutional credibility for provenance ("we e-mailed our solutions privately to the FirstProof authors at 11:07pm PST") | We have no institutional credibility. The cryptographic chain must do the work that DeepMind's reputation does. |
| Novel inference-time scaling laws ("orders of magnitude" beyond IMO-Gold) | We use standard API-accessible inference. No custom scaling infrastructure. |
| Extensive internal benchmarks (FutureMath Basic, IMO-ProofBench Advanced) | We have no internal benchmarks. We rely on the community to develop evaluation suites (§7.1). |

### §10. Relationship to the Publishing Ecosystem

#### 10.1 Traditional Publishers

The factory is designed to produce output that is **compatible
with** but **not automatically compliant with** the AI
disclosure requirements of major publishers. The HAI Card
(D-6 §7) maps to specific publisher requirements:

| Publisher | What the Factory Provides | What the Human Must Still Do |
|---|---|---|
| Elsevier | The HAI Card serves as the "AI declaration statement." The provenance chain provides the audit trail. | The human must independently verify all citations. The human must sign the disclosure attestation. The human must not submit AI-generated images. |
| Springer Nature | The HAI Card content can be incorporated into the Methods section. | The human must review and rewrite the core text to the extent required by the publisher's prohibition on direct AI output. |
| Wiley | The HAI Card provides the "transparent and detailed" description of AI usage. | The human must verify that the AI tool's terms of service do not create IP conflicts. |
| Taylor & Francis | The HAI Card provides the explicit acknowledgement of AI tool name and purpose. | The human must take full accountability. |

**The factory does NOT automate submission to any venue.**
There is no "submit" button. There is no API integration with
journal submission systems. The human operator must manually
review the output, verify the claims, and submit through
normal channels. This is by design.

#### 10.2 JAIGP

The Journal for AI Generated Papers (JAIGP) is the most
natural venue for factory output. The factory's architecture
directly supports JAIGP's multi-stage pipeline:

| JAIGP Stage | Factory Support |
|---|---|
| Automated AI Screening | The HAI Card and provenance manifest provide structured metadata for automated screening. |
| Human ORCID Endorsement | The `reviewer_id` field in the HAI Card (D-6 §4.4) accepts ORCIDs. |
| Automated AI Review (Reviewer3.com) | The Verifier's `VerifierOutput` (D-2 §8.4) can be exported as supplementary material for Reviewer3.com. |
| Human Peer Review | The full workspace provides the raw material for human reviewers. |

**The project recommends JAIGP as the primary publication
venue for factory output until the "slop" designation is
removed.** Submission to traditional venues should occur
only when a human domain expert has independently verified
the scientific claims and is willing to take authorship
accountability.

#### 10.3 The arXiv Question

arXiv is a preprint server, not a peer-reviewed journal.
It does not require human authorship and does not prohibit
AI-generated content. However, arXiv does require that a
submitting author "vouch for the intellectual content" of
the submission.

**The project takes no position on whether factory output
should be posted to arXiv.** This is a decision for the
individual operator, who must comply with arXiv's policies
and take personal responsibility for the content.

If factory output is posted to arXiv:
1. The HAI Card MUST be included as an appendix.
2. The provenance manifest MUST be linked from the paper.
3. The full workspace MUST be published at a public URL.
4. The abstract MUST include the sentence: "This document
   was produced by the antiphoria slop-research-factory,
   an autonomous AI pipeline. No human domain expert has
   verified the scientific claims."

### §11. Governance Structure

#### 11.1 Technical Steering Committee (TSC)

The project is governed by a Technical Steering Committee
of 3–7 members. The initial TSC is the project's founding
contributors.

**TSC responsibilities:**
- Approve or reject changes to D-0 through D-9.
- Approve new dependencies.
- Enforce the communication standards (§5).
- Decide on the removal of the "slop" designation (§2.4).
- Handle Code of Conduct violations (§6.3).
- Approve official project communications (§3.3).

**TSC decision-making:**
- Routine decisions (bug fixes, minor documentation):
  1 TSC member approval.
- Significant decisions (new D-document versions, new
  model integrations, dependency additions):
  2 TSC member approval.
- Governance decisions (charter amendments, TSC membership
  changes, "slop" designation removal):
  75% supermajority of all TSC members.

#### 11.2 TSC Membership

TSC members are added by unanimous consent of the existing
TSC. TSC members may resign at any time. A TSC member may
be removed by a 75% supermajority vote of the remaining
TSC members, for:
- Repeated violations of the communication standards (§5).
- Failure to participate in governance decisions for 6
  consecutive months.
- Actions that bring the project into disrepute.

#### 11.3 Community Advisors

The project may invite Community Advisors: domain experts,
ethicists, publishers, or other stakeholders who provide
non-binding advice to the TSC. Community Advisors have no
voting power but may participate in TSC discussions.

The project SHOULD seek Community Advisors from:
- Academic mathematics (to calibrate the significance axis).
- Academic publishing (to ensure compliance with
  evolving policies).
- Cryptography and security (to audit the provenance
  system).
- AI ethics (to evaluate the social implications of the
  tool).

#### 11.4 Governance Review

Any community member may request a governance review by
filing an issue in the project repository with the
`governance-review` label. The TSC must respond within
14 days. If the issue concerns a TSC member, that member
recuses themselves from the discussion.

### §12. Licensing

#### 12.1 Software License

The factory source code is released under the **Apache
License 2.0**.

Rationale: Apache 2.0 provides:
- Freedom to use, modify, and redistribute.
- Patent protection for contributors and users.
- No copyleft requirement (organizations can integrate
  the factory into proprietary workflows).
- Compatibility with most other open-source licenses.

#### 12.2 Output License and Disclaimer Requirement

The factory's output (generated papers, HAI Cards, provenance
manifests) is released under **CC BY 4.0 (Creative Commons
Attribution 4.0 International)** with the following additional
condition:

> **Any public use of factory output must preserve the
> disclaimer block (D-6 §4.1) and the HAI Card (D-6 §3)
> in their entirety.** Removal of the disclaimer or HAI Card
> from publicly distributed factory output constitutes a
> violation of the license terms.

This is implemented as a **binding license condition**, not
merely a polite request. The CC BY 4.0 license requires
attribution; we define "attribution" to include the
disclaimer and HAI Card.

**Rationale:** The disclaimer and HAI Card are the primary
mechanisms by which the project prevents epistemic harm
(D-1 §3, Adversary 5). Making them removable defeats their
purpose.

#### 12.3 Documentation License

The D-documents (D-0 through D-9, including this charter)
are released under **CC BY-SA 4.0 (Creative Commons
Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International)**.

Rationale: The ShareAlike clause ensures that derivative
governance documents (e.g., adapted for other projects)
must remain open. The project's governance model is itself
a contribution to the community and should propagate.

#### 12.4 Provenance Engine

The provenance seal engine (`slop-cli` or equivalent) may
have its own license independent of the factory. The factory
treats the seal engine as an external dependency (D-5 §2)
and makes no licensing claims over it.

### §13. The Science Fiction Clause

This section exists because the project's founder explicitly
framed the work as "science fiction we are about to build."

The factory is simultaneously two things:

1. **An engineering artifact that works today.** The code runs.
   The seals hash. The Verifier outputs structured critiques.
   The HAI Card generates. The provenance chain links. These
   are concrete, testable, deployable capabilities.

2. **A speculation about what research could become.** The
   factory embodies a bet: that within 3–5 years, the
   verification taxonomy will mature, the models will
   improve, and the output will cross the threshold from
   slop to science. The architecture is designed to survive
   that transition unchanged.

The project embraces both identities. We do not pretend the
factory produces science today. We do not pretend the
architecture is merely academic. We are building the
scaffolding for a building that does not yet exist, and we
are honest about the fact that the scaffolding is currently
holding up air.

**The Science Fiction Clause grants the project permission
to be optimistic about the future while being ruthlessly
honest about the present.** Contributors and users who
cannot hold both truths simultaneously — who insist either
that the factory is already producing research or that the
project is pointless because it cannot — may be better
served by other projects.

The tension between aspiration and honesty is not a bug.
It is the project's reason for existing.
